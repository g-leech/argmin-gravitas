One great Turing biographer, Andrew Hodges, says it "seemed obvious" that his death was suicide;

> By the side of his bed was half an apple, out of which several bites had been taken. They did not analyse the apple, and so it was never properly established that, as seemed perfectly obvious, the apple had been dipped in the cyanide.

Another, [Jack Copeland](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/av/science-environment-18562183), argues that the evidence is more consistent with accidental poisoning (inhalation).

The inquest into the death was hasty and incompetent. For instance, the supposed suicide weapon, a poisoned apple, was never tested for poison. And Turing's family chose not to contest the verdict, for reasons nothing to do with the evidence <a href="#fn:9" id="fnref:9">9</a>

Hodges is thorough: I got most of the counterevidence below from his book. But, among its other offences the <i>film</i> of his book wholesale invents a mental breakdown and teary confession of despair. It invents a story about the police suspecting him of being a Soviet spy. It shows him covering up for a Soviet spy, John Cairncross; i.e. being a coward and traitor. It shows him building a computer named for his first love, in an idiotic _Frankenstein_ sense. It shows him having a breakdown after the hormone punishment. It hypes the relationship with Joan Clarke. It shows him being interrogated about his homosexuality, when actually he wrote a five-page confession without much prompting, his contribution to gay rights: self-sacrifice as protest.

This doesn't fit.

<h2>A setup</h2>

We want a probability for an event which only happened once, so it's Bayes. Two live hypotheses <a href="#fn:1" id="fnref:1">1</a>:

<ul>
	<li>\(S\): he poisoned himself on purpose.</li>
	<li>\(A\): he poisoned himself by accident — inhaling the vapour off the electrolysis rig bubbling in the next room, or carrying it hand-to-mouth.</li>
</ul>

In odds form, so that I never have to compute a denominator:

$$ \underbrace{\frac{P(S \mid E)}{P(A \mid E)}}_{\text{posterior odds}} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{P(S)}{P(A)}}_{\text{prior odds}} \;\times\; \underbrace{\frac{P(E \mid S)}{P(E \mid A)}}_{\text{likelihood ratio}} $$

<br>

<h3>1. The base rates do not give us the prior odds</h3>

In the early 1950s, the suicide rate among British men in their early forties was
<a href="https://oup.silverchair-cdn.com/oup/backfile/Content_public/Journal/ije/39/6/10.1093_ije_dyq094/1/m_dyq094f3a.gif?Expires=1505678487&Signature=VCigV0ecvJUK5eIzdFDpm4hEH5gGdVCliOPEL0K8LZDwy9LYr8Tr~qHYcxZEIn8r7dcADLJIxiRCqDIimLxhsuYu-KD~uMmJIFffJr6xyPhl59eocUAnRSHV1TjsKGp6sptga4VTuOzFoUNxNZF2ENkLJxt6~TnfAh~3cmkxSu024m-UPF-LbQpKC9GxsopuaK1JAev0jbxTLIFSnYPgTia7q3OUgi0OWrJ3FtKjbxUJZ1tpm5QIgc83umqnkKB42mPQ6pWbg2A1aIm538efRON2GQJJUc0W-UQfhsCbOGSQH1eLPlWvIeDrOOnCd6uv9NCd2~4c~tqguKSdvLXS7A__&Key-Pair-Id=APKAIUCZBIA4LVPAVW3Q">12 per 100,000</a> per year. To my knowledge there are no studies of suicide among gay men in the 50s, but in the 90s gay men in the US were <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1447240/ ">roughly three times</a> as likely to attempt suicide as straight men. <a href="#fn:2" id="fnref:2">2</a> The most relevant subpopulation — gay men convicted for having consensual sex and then put on diethylstilbestrol — is a blank page.

Having been found dead, the probability of the cause being suicide was about 1% for a man like Turing (41 in 1954). <a href="#fn:3" id="fnref:3">3</a>

<br>

I used to think you could race the suicide rate against the accidental-poisoning rate <a href="#fn:4" id="fnref:4">4</a> and read an answer off. 

Almost all of the population accident rate is generated by people with no access to cyanide. Conditioning on <i>died of cyanide</i> selects almost completely <i>out</i> of that reference class — and Turing is one of the few thousand men in Britain that it selects <i>into</i>. So the population ratio isn't Turing's prior odds. It's the prior odds for a man unlike Turing in the key respect.

<br>

The hazard following a disgrace like this is sharply front-loaded. It peaks in weeks, not years. Turing died 26 months after the conviction and around 14 months after the stilboestrol course ended. Whatever \(m\) was in March 1952, it is smaller in June 1954 — and Hodges himself reports no decline in his intellectual life, a <i>wider</i> range of interests, and "a more open attitude to intellectual and emotional life". So \(m=30\) is very hard to defend at the moment of death. Carry \(m \in [3, 10]\).

<br>

<h3>3. His accident hazard</h3>

There is no reference class for this.

<ul>
	<li>He kept potassium cyanide in the house, for gold-plating.</li>
	<li>A rig was bubbling away in an unventilated box room — which still smelled strongly of cyanide <i>after</i> his death.</li>
	<li>He tasted chemicals to identify them.</li>
	<li>He didn't wash his hands. His mother's standing instruction, repeated at their last Christmas together: "<i>Wash your hands, Alan, and get your nails clean. And don't put your fingers in your mouth!</i>"</li>
	<li>He wired the mains into a ceiling light socket, and had already given himself severe shocks doing it.</li>
</ul>

So don't estimate it. <b>Solve for it.</b> <a href="#fn:5" id="fnref:5">5</a> Given death by cyanide,

$$ \text{prior odds}(S{:}A) \;=\; \frac{c \cdot \lambda_S}{\lambda_A} $$

where \(c = P(\text{cyanide is the method} \mid \text{Turing suicides})\). He has it to hand, and had described an apple-and-wiring scheme twenty years earlier, so \(c\) is high for him. Call it 0.5.

Now set the odds to 1 and ask what \(\lambda_A\) would have to be for the two hypotheses to tie:

<br>

And that is the whole base-rate question, reduced to something a person could actually go and answer:

<blockquote>
Does a man who tastes unknown chemicals, won't wash his hands, does shit like wiring the mains power into his light fitting, and leaves potassium cyanide evaporating in an unventilated room have more than a <b>1-in-1,000</b> chance per year of killing himself with it by accident?
</blockquote>

I say yes, comfortably. But the real gain is that this is now a question about amateur chemists and electroplaters. <a href="#fn:6" id="fnref:6">6</a>

Note the direction of the result. It takes only a 1-in-1,000-a-year clumsiness hazard to cancel a <i>tenfold</i> post-conviction suicide multiplier. The prior is not where this case gets decided, and everyone who has tried to decide it there — in both directions, me included — was wasting their breath. So: agnostic prior, 1:1, and make the evidence do the work.

	<h3>His method</h3>
	<div>
		Bayes is much older than Turing, but the bookkeeping I just did (score evidence as likelihood ratios, take logs to <i>add</i> them, accumulate until you know something) is Turing's <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banburismus">Banburismus</a> method, invented around 1940 to strip wheel orders off of Naval Enigma. He needed a unit for weight of evidence, so he made one: the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ban_(unit)">ban</a>, \(\log_{10}\) of a likelihood ratio, named for the Banbury sheets they were scored on. A deciban is a tenth of that, and was reckoned to be about the smallest weight of evidence a person can actually feel.<br><br>

		The hardest quantity in this post is \(\lambda_A\): the rate of an event observed precisely once, with no reference class.
