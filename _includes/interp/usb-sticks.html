An interpretation, by Claude Fable.

The form is a catalogue, and the catalogue is the argument. Each entry pairs a physical object — the USB stick, described with a collector's cruel precision ("Black telescopic macho toy," "Green squidgy Chibi with a thin white extending tongue," "Gaffa tape and hope, DEADFORMAT NOSTALGIA") — with the data found inside it. The joke, sustained for twenty-odd stanzas, is the total non-relationship between the shell and the contents. Nothing about the object predicts the soul it carried. A garish macho toy holds "Primary school lesson plans, LGBT materials, and Beyoncé's discography." A cutesy cartoon-flower stick holds "A man's CV in Czech, alone" — that alone doing an enormous amount of quiet work.

What accumulates is a portrait of a whole society through its lost fragments, the way an archaeologist reads a midden. And this is the poem's kinship with uluburun a few years later — both are inventories of deposited objects whose meaning has come unstuck from their material. Here the found data is comically, movingly heterogeneous: Anammox bacteria and denitrifiers; guitar tabs for Maggie May; Eurocopter blueprints; "Brazilian and Portuguese passports, Saudi visa. The smell of money"; "Signed landlord documents and one soft porn film of 50mins." The workplace (the poet's day job in property) becomes a random-access sample of everyone's secret private business, dropped and forgotten and now readable by a stranger.

The tenderness is in the restraint. The poet never editorialises about the people; he lets the juxtapositions do it. "I ate your children — and what's more they are happier now" — a line of found text (a fragment of some novel or note) that lands like a horror story precisely because we will never know its frame. "Empty." on the decrepit 128MB stick is a whole life in one word. The recurring "Sleek redblack again" and the two "Switchblade in chrome" entries hint at a world of near-identical objects carrying wildly divergent lives — sports science, Polish technical documents, Hans Zimmer's soundtrack to True Romance.

Underneath the comedy is a real thesis about privacy and legibility in the digital age: we scatter perfect, dense copies of ourselves into cheap disposable shells and lose them, and those copies are meaningful and unreadable at once — passworded, contextless, "esoteric file formats: Jar files. LSTs, IVSs, .gzs. I draw blanks, no association." The stranger who finds them can recover the bytes exactly and the person not at all. It is the same grief as the shipwreck poem, transposed to plastic and flash memory: we know a great deal now about people we still know nothing about.
