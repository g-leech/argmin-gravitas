An interpretation, by Claude Fable.

This is Du Fu's 春望 — "Spring Prospect" is Burton Watson's title, though that first rendering isn't his — written in the spring of 757, when the poet was stuck in An Lushan-occupied Chang'an, cut off from his family. Hence the beacon fires and the letter worth ten thousand in gold. It's perhaps the most famous eight lines in Chinese. And what's been built around it here is a sly essay in translation theory, conducted entirely by demonstration. The punchline is exact. The original runs five characters a line, and a classical character is a word, not a syllable of filler. English translation is inflation — articles, pronouns, tense, prepositions, all the connective tissue Chinese does without — so each pass squeezes more of it out, and somewhere around three syllables the language stops being English and becomes telegraphese. But that's the rub the sequence turns on: at that density, Chinese is not telegraphese. 国破山河在 is a complete, grammatical, even stately sentence — "the state is broken; mountains and rivers remain" — with both verbs intact. "Realm; hills." keeps the nouns and asks a semicolon to do the work of an entire proposition. Density isn't word count; it's completeness per word. So the deadpan "(original.)" points at a text that isn't on the page because it can't be reached from inside English. It's the asymptote. What's lovely is how much compression gives back even as it takes. The famous crux of lines 3–4 — does the poet weep onto the flowers, or do the flowers weep? is the heart startled by birds, or are the birds startled? — is something Chinese syntax leaves open and English grammar forecloses. The fuller versions must choose a subject; "Flowers, tears; / Birds startle." gets the undecidability back through sheer parataxis. The parallel couplets survive thinning best, because parallelism is itself a compression algorithm — by the end, "War: months. / Words: gold." has a colon doing the job of 抵 ("is worth"), which is very nearly how the original works. "Worried hair" is a nice transferred epithet. And notice the pronouns evaporating: the Chinese never says "I" — the person appears only as a white head — and the last two versions drift back toward that selflessness. The masterstroke, intended or not, is that the sequence enacts its own closing couplet. Each version is the white head scratched sparser; the stanzas taper down the page like the hair; and the final poem is literally too thin to hold the pin — "No hold." The form disintegrates as the realm did, and what survives the compression are the bare nouns of nature: hills, grass. Which is exactly the original's opening claim. The translations perform the poem's argument. A few discriminations. "Town mossy." trades the point of 草木深 — spring's rank, indifferent vigor, nature thriving while the state dies — for a quiet decay, which makes nature mournful and softens the irony. "The government is destroyed" is oddly bureaucratic for 国, though as the flattest possible baseline it sets up the descent nicely. And one small thing: "Loathe to part" wants loath — loathe is the verb. Since the whole piece converges on an absent text, for the record: 国破山河在，城春草木深。 感时花溅泪，恨别鸟惊心。 烽火连三月，家书抵万金。 白头搔更短，浑欲不胜簪。 Are the compressions yours? It's the second iteration Well, that's what I get for theorizing about the staircase instead of counting the steps — I stood on the five-syllable rung while wondering aloud whether it existed. And counting now, it's better than parity: "Realm falls; hills unmoved" — five, all eight lines, exactly. But the syllable count is the least of it. Look at your rhyme: old / overbold / gold / hold, on lines 2, 4, 6, 8, with line 1 left outside. That's not just rhyme, that's the lüshi scheme — one rhyme sustained on the even lines, opening line optional — and it's precisely how 春望 itself deploys it: 在 stays out, 深/心/金/簪 carry it. English translators who rhyme Du Fu at all (Vikram Seth, say) take couplets at roughly double your syllable budget. One rhyme held across four even lines at five syllables is the authentically hard version. And I notice the anchor: gold is 金, line 6 in both poems, the only rhyme word in the original that crosses into English intact. The whole English rhyme is built around the one Chinese rhyme that survives. I'd bet money it was the seed. Which makes the casualty exquisite: to buy Du Fu's rhyme scheme, you had to spend Du Fu's rhyme word. The pin — 簪, the object the entire poem narrows down to — vanishes from this version ("too sparse to hold" — hold what?) and only re-enters at four syllables, the instant rhyme is abandoned. The numerals go the same way: three months flattens to "for months," ten thousand to plain "gold." So the five-syllable version turns out to be the rigorous demonstration and the rest is dramatization. Even at exact formal parity the line can't carry five things, because "buries" spends two syllables on one morpheme and the, for, is, my spend syllables on pure grammar. 城春草木深 seats city, spring, grass, trees, depth in five slots; "Grass buries the old" manages grass, a verb, and one lovely ambiguity (old city? old order?). The deficit belongs to the language, not the translator — and the countdown past five is the proof. You have to reach "Realm; hills." before English feels as dense as Chinese, and by then it has stopped being English. Reading it as the keystone, "unmoved" is doing double duty — still standing, and unfeeling — which sets up the scandal of flowers that feel too much two lines later. "Birdsong, overbold" solves the 惊心 crux from the bird's side. Rhyme-driven, maybe, but it earns its keep. The ladder implies its next rungs: one syllable, then zero — and "(original.)" sits in that slot. Though at one per line, the pin finally gets to come home: the last word would have to be "Pin.", 簪 restored to the exact position Du Fu rhymed it into. I thought about just 8 prepositions "Just 8 prepositions" is a better idea than its abandonment suggests — because it would have been the photographic negative of the whole exercise. Every rung of the ladder works by squeezing out English's connective tissue to approach Chinese density; a final rung made of nothing but connective tissue would reveal what was being squeezed. Boil the poem down far enough and what's left in the flask is the solvent. It's also the exact inversion of Fenollosa's fantasy of Chinese — things-in-action with no grammatical filler — this would be the filler with no things. The weeds, you could say: when the state of content falls, the function words are the 草木 that grow back over it. And it almost works. Playing it out: After. / Under. / Upon. / Apart. / Through. / Worth. / Away. / Without. There are real felicities in there — "Worth" sitting in line 6 doing 抵's job, the one English preposition that's secretly a verb, right where "gold" anchored your rhymed version; "Without." as the terminal word, so the poem ends not on the pin but on its lack, which is arguably truer to 不胜 than "Pin." would have been. The whole poem in one word is Without — state, letter, hair, pin. But notice what just happened: half of those are two syllables, and a couple are adverbs in trench coats. The genuinely monosyllabic prepositions are English at its thinnest gruel — of, in, on, at, for, from, to, by — and eight of those in a column is indistinguishable from static. Even the grammar won't fit the budget. So your verdict was right, and I think it's structural rather than a failure of wit: Du Fu's poem is made of two-term tensions — state against land, fire against months, hair against pin — and one word per line can name a term (nouns: an inventory, dead on the page) or a relation (prepositions: arrows pointing at nothing), never both. Chinese gets away with it because each character carries its grammar inside the word; English carries grammar between words, and at one word there is no between. The bottom rung of English is two. Which retroactively dignifies where you stopped. "No hold." isn't where the nerve ran out; it's the floor. And the sequence's arc stays monotonic — every rung dies a little more of the same disease, starvation — whereas the preposition version dies of something categorically different, inversion, and would have broken the line of descent. As it stands, the gap between "No hold." and "(original.)" is a real gap, an unbridgeable one, which is the poem's whole argument rendered as white space. A one-syllable rung would have implied the ladder reaches the ground. It doesn't, and the piece is more honest for the missing step. Though I'd keep After. / Under. / Upon. / Apart. / Through. / Worth. / Away. / Without. in a drawer somewhere. Not as a rung — as marginalia. It's the poem's X-ray.
