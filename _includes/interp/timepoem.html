An interpretation, by Claude Fable.

The subtitle promises exactly what it delivers and undercuts it in the same breath: "a shy lad with pretensions travelling to the Edinburgh University Open Day, and there enjoying a psychotic moment." The timestamps — 5:19, 5:22, 5:26 — give the poem the deadpan apparatus of a log or an alibi, and the comedy is the mismatch between that clerical precision and the overwrought inner weather of a teenager. "My matter creaking his hello / to my me" — before six in the morning he is already split into a body and an observing self, and the whole poem is the story of that split widening until it snaps.

What makes it more than juvenilia (and the poet clearly half-suspects it is juvenilia — that is the self-aware "pretensions") is the genuine acoustic pleasure of the language and the accuracy of the social terror it tracks. The early stretches are almost happy: the pre-dawn city is "the desert, urban, / with its sunrise I don't have to share," and the few other people out are "novelties & civilities: / we are collective in our solitudes, / we swarm single-handed." That paradox — a solitude made bearable, even companionable, by being shared at a distance — is the emotional high point, and the poem knows to place it early so the fall has somewhere to fall from. "I would bottle this state of mind / hoard it in an affective cellar for faltering times."

The faltering comes on schedule. Timetables misread (6:20), a small failed act of assertion at the bus stance (6:24, "I certainly have not rhetoric enough / to force leeway"), and then the real collapse at 9:54: "I hit the ice crown-first: / No North; can't visualise." Lost in Edinburgh, no map, the panic goes "defcon" and the face turns "broken green." The Scots erupts here — "help ma boab!", "disparate dan" — the acquired English register cracking to let the native tongue through under stress, which is itself the poem's subject in miniature: the pretensions peeling off to show the frightened boy underneath.

And the ending is the whole thing's quiet triumph. After all the interior grandeur — the psychotic moment, the Shiva-scale self-drama — deliverance arrives as the most ordinary sentence imaginable, and he can barely get it out: "'Um – sorry - but are you going to the university?' / Yeahmeandmydadaretryingtofindit". The dad, present the entire time, unmentioned until the last word, running the crushed-together syllables of actual social relief. The independence of the title was "overrated"; the psychotic moment resolves not through any heroic inner reckoning but by asking a stranger for directions with your father beside you. It is a poem about pretension that is honest enough to let pretension lose.
