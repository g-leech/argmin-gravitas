An interpretation, by Claude Fable.

The setup is a joke the poem never quite admits is a joke: the only beautiful thing near Riga airport is industrial exhaust — "the spew of a squat smokestack, / a pure grey stream of hot sideshow." A traveller, "youngish half-educated," has half an hour to kill and finds himself moved by a plume of pollution. Everything that follows is the poem interrogating that small, embarrassing pulse of pleasure until it opens onto the whole question of what science leaves standing of beauty.

"Parthenogenetic and progeroid" — born without a mate, aged before its time. The smokestack's output is sterile and dying at once, a monochrome that washes the winter sky out to "the static milk-blue back of this." And then the turn that the poem is built around: "and I could study this! Really." The vapour is not mysterious. It obeys "the eternal manner since Boyle or Navier-Stokes"; a chemist friend "can code its dead language in a trice"; it "solves for seven unknowns." The poem knows exactly how thoroughly the thing before it has been explained. The question is whether being explained empties it.

This is the old Romantic anxiety — Keats's complaint that Newton unwove the rainbow — but the poem refuses the Romantic answer. It will not pretend the physics is a desecration. Instead it does something braver and stranger: it locates the beauty and the pleasure and the observer all inside the same describable world. "The present effect — one unit of pleasure and motive / in a youngish half-educated traveller — is known, shakily, in principle, / from parts outside: sparkling in my C fibres or Hebbian knots or GABA wheels." The delight is as reducible as the smoke. There is no exempt spectator standing outside the equations; even "the heart may be knowable / given much stronger light."

And yet the poem's texture keeps outrunning its own reductionism. "On average each twin seen truly. / we found out the world without us. made it reel." That is not a defeated line — it is exultant. The models "lift off, freed from obvious / falsehood by tiny effects in giant interactions / so we think what we happily will." The honest admission is that macro-facts (the temperature and health of Riga, the traveller's own mood) are "knowable but not known and not soon" — the science exists but no one has run the numbers, and won't, and in that gap "we think what we happily will." The poem's final posture is neither disenchantment nor its opposite. It is a kind of grateful humility: standing in a place designed to have no meaning — "the deep absence / that airports consist in" — watching a cloud factory that "ignores my head, dipped in notebook; ignores the folly and doom / symbolism people give it," caring for nothing but "pressures, gravities, van der Waals // and future soon." Beauty survives being explained. It just stops being about us, and the poem decides it can live with that.
