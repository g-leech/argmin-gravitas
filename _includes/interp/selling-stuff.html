An interpretation, by Claude Fable.

This is "Lähen müüjaks" ("I'll Go Work as a Shopgirl"), written in 1965 — i.e. not a 1930s poem but a late retrospective one, from the collection that ended Alver's roughly two-decade public silence under Soviet rule. It's an autobiographical vignette of her childhood home — she grew up essentially in the Jõgeva railway station house, her father a railway official, and the text is a picture of that childhood, so Leihberg is the actual local shopkeeper ("our Leihberg's shop" in the original final line), not an allegorical figure.

The poem is spoken by a hungry child, and its engine is the gap between what the child sees and what the child does with it. The first half is an inventory of want, rendered almost cosily because the speaker is too young to editorialise: the kettle hums but "our pan's got no pancakes on"; "My belly's been empty for ages"; the mother is not idle but improvising, "cutting up a leftover boot to make me some shoes in a batch." The father reads the newspaper — Africa, Rothschild, the King of Angels, an earthquake in Turkey — the whole wide catastrophic world arriving as print while the immediate catastrophe is that there is nothing to eat. "As I thin the prices grow fatter" is the poem's sharpest economic line, and it comes from a mouth that doesn't yet know it's making an argument.

Then, at "Guess I too could settle down in sales for a Leihberg wage," the poem detonates into fantasy, and everything changes register. The child's response to poverty is not despair but a manic, joyous vision of abundance — becoming a shopkeeper, a dealer in everything. And notice what the fantasy is made of: sound. "sell almonds and raisins by backhoe — haphazard and heapwise in gluts!" "Sell socks sell boots sell shawls, and saucepans for soups and for sauces." The alliteration piles up like the goods themselves; the poem starts selling by the sackful and the line lengths bulge to hold it. This is Betti Alver — one of the great Estonian formalists — writing a child's daydream as pure prosodic surplus, the rhythm doing what the pantry can't, filling the space with plenty.

The fantasy is also, quietly, a fantasy of generosity, which is what makes it more than escapism. The imagined shop gives things away: "Half-off — all off — forever my guy, use fallen leaves for the money!" Buy sprats and get herrings thrown in; your skirt in tatters, no matter, the cloth shelf is sublime. A child who has nothing dreams not of hoarding but of a world where goods "dance to the shop-counter hop" and abundance overflows onto everyone. The scarcity of the first half is answered not with acquisition but with an imagined cornucopia to be scattered.

And then Alver puts the pin in, gently, with two words. The boss "will come and thank us like a man!... Perhaps." That Perhaps — with its ellipsis, its small deflation — is the adult's knowledge leaking back into the child's rapture. Maybe Mister Leihberg won't thank anyone; maybe the wage won't come; maybe the whole shop is fallen leaves. But the poem doesn't end on the doubt. It ends on the invitation: "Come, friends, to the shop party! Surely come! See yas chaps!" The dream reasserts itself over the perhaps, the way a child's does, and the last note is hospitality — the empty-bellied host throwing open the doors. It is a poem about poverty that refuses to be a poem about despair, and the refusal is itself the meaning.

The poem is built on one hinge. The first half is an inventory of lack rendered as substitution: sound without substance (the kettle hums, the pan is empty), presence without contact (mother there but turned away, recutting a boot into shoes; father there but absorbed in the newspaper). The second half answers this economy of scarcity with its exact inverse — but crucially, not by rejecting commerce. The child keeps the entire apparatus of the shop (stock, patter, prices, the wage, the boss) and hollows out its core: quantities go absurd ("by backhoe... in gluts"), the inventory drifts from staples to luxuries to outright toys ("herds of cardboard horses"), prices collapse ("Half-off — all off, forever"), and money is replaced by leaves. In the original the currency is lepalehed, alder leaves — the standard play-money of Estonian children's shop games — so the whole second half is literally a child playing shop, projected at full scale onto the adult world. The dream is of helping everyone in need half-free, entirely free, for alder leaves. The critique works by overperformance rather than denunciation: this is Bakhtin's carnivalesque marketplace, abundance and levelling delivered in the real oral genre of the market-crier's spiel, which the verse itself imitates (the galloping internal rhymes, the vocatives, the call-and-response distribution — sweat gets soap, sighs get herrings, tatters get sublime cloth). The first half deserves close reading on its own terms. Newspaper consumption is staged as liturgy: "a Rich Man the King of Angels, amen" is a mock-doxology, the father worshipping at the altar of finance (Rothschild as the era's synecdoche for capital). "The rain speaks Latin" extends this — the adult, institutional world is an unintelligible sacral language, heard from below by a hungry child; this is estrangement via child focalization in Shklovsky's sense. The flattening "too" in "In Turkey the earth's shaking, too" is doing double work: it satirizes how the press equalizes distant catastrophe with the weather, and it honestly reports the child's scale of values, on which her stomach and an Anatolian earthquake are equally weighted facts. And "As I thin the prices grow fatter" is the poem's thesis in a chiasmus: an inverse metabolism in which the market feeds on the body. The ending then punctures the game with a single word. Everything in the fantasy is asserted in the confident future tense except one thing — Mister Leihberg's gratitude, which gets "…Perhaps." The child half-knows that giving the shop away is the ruin of the shop; the gift economy and the wage economy are incompatible, and the only place they coexist is in play. The line "The bigguns have their big boots. I'd rather stay small and meander" states the resulting ethic outright: against the scale-logic of boots (note the footwear chain — boot cut down to shoes, big boots, boots for sale), smallness, drift, and play. Two framing notes. Written in 1965, the poem wears a censor-friendly genre — memories of capitalist-era poverty — while its actual energy is the imagination's insubordination to any economic logic; a fantasy of overflowing shelves and goods given away would have read with a distinct second irony in a Soviet deficit economy, an Aesopian doubling Alver's generation used routinely. That second reading is speculative but cheap to hold alongside the first. And the poem has had an afterlife: Vaiko Eplik built his 2024 album "Lähen müüjaks" around it, noting he happened to read it the week of the big Turkish earthquake and finding it strikingly contemporary — the recurrence of "wages won't see you through" being the obvious reason. Quite a few — and the pattern is consistent: the translation intensifies. Where Alver is deadpan and paratactic, the English is explicit, violent, or festive. Ranked by interpretive weight: "As I thin the prices grow fatter" is invented. The original (l.10) is "Kõik aetakse kole kalliks" — "everything is being driven terribly dear." No thinning body, no fattening prices, no chiasmus. Two things are lost and one gained: lost is the impersonal passive aetakse ("is driven"), which implies an agent pushing prices up rather than prices organically "growing"; lost is the plainness; gained is a body–market metabolism the translator built by splicing in the hunger from l.4. The hunger line itself is the idiom "Mu kere on ammugi hele" — literally "my frame has long been bright/ringing," the Estonian idiom for famished — whose synaesthetic strangeness "my belly's been empty for ages" flattens. The Rothschild blasphemy is forced, and "amen" is added. "Rotschildist, rikkast mehest ja inglite kuningast" reads most naturally as a list of newspaper items: of Rothschild, the rich man, and of the King of Angels (God) — i.e. finance news and church news sitting side by side in the same paper. The satire is by adjacency. The English collapses the apposition into an equation (Rothschild = Rich Man = King of Angels) and seals it with an "amen" that is nowhere in the Estonian. "Rothschild's latest views" is also invented (rhyme-driven; the original has news about him). There is no party. The final line is "Tulge, sõbrad, Leihbergi poodi!" — "Come, friends, to Leihberg's shop!" — followed by the standard shop-assistant's sign-off "Nägemist!" (goodbye). The original ends still inside the sales-call genre, deadpan to the last word; "Come, friends, to the party!" literalises a carnival frame Alver leaves implicit. The English also drops one of the original's three closing Leihberg repetitions ("Leihbergi lett" → "the shop-counter"). "Fallen leaves" for "lepalehtede" (alder leaves). As suspected: the original names the specific species that is the conventional play-money of Estonian children's shop games — picked green, coin-like — not autumn litter. "Forever" and the instruction "use … for the money" are both additions; the original is just the tariff: "half-for-nothing, entirely free, and for alder leaves." The drum migrates and the weather turns violent. Alver's kettle is a "teetrummel" — literally a "tea-drum" — humming as it boils; her wind merely "rabistab ruutudele raagusid puiesteelt": patters bare twigs against the windowpanes from the avenue, a delicate, heavily r-alliterated acoustic line. The translator moved the drum from kettle to wind and escalated to "rages" and "marshals branches" — martial semantics replacing onomatopoeia, with the panes (the point of the image) deleted. Line 1 similarly: "Must vari käib … üle me köögilae" is a black shadow that habitually passes over the kitchen ceiling; "something comes … gone" makes it indefinite and momentary, and drops the kitchen. "And meander" is added. The original is just "Ma parem jään väikeseks" — "I'd rather stay small." The drift/wandering ethic is the translator's. "For a Leihberg wage" replaces "meie Leihbergi poes" — "in our Leihberg's shop." The possessive marks Leihberg as the familiar village shopkeeper; the English abstracts him into an employer-brand and imports the wage theme into a line that doesn't have it. The abundance is inflated and the texture coarsened. "In sackfuls," "by backhoe," "in gluts," "herds of" (cardboard horses), "sublime" (for "a whole shelf full of cloth"), "snap-snap" — none present; the original's absurdity is carried by two adverbs, "huupi ja hulgaviisi" (haphazardly and in bulk). Smaller slips: "präänikuid suhkrust ja meest" is gingerbread made of sugar and honey, not three commodities; "suuri heeringaid" is big herrings (the size upgrade is the joke), not "herrings by lot"; "pupenett" is bobbinet/tulle — the luxury pole opposite coarse canvas — not dimity. Formally, ABAB short-line quatrains become long couplets, and the eye-dialect ("gonna," "em," "bigguns," "See yas chaps") has no counterpart: Alver's Estonian is orthographically standard throughout, the child signalled by diction and logic, not spelling. Even "Vist" is arguably closer to "probably, I suppose" than to "Perhaps" — the child mostly believes. This obliges a correction of my previous reading. The mock-doxology, the thin/fat chiasmus, the meander, the feast-closure, and the flattening "too" (the original twice has bare "Ja…", whose parataxis does a quieter version of that work) were all claims about the translator's poem, not Alver's. What survives in the Estonian: the play-shop fantasy priced in alder leaves, the need-matched gifts, the wage couplet, smallness against big boots, the "Vist" deflation, and satire-by-juxtaposition rather than by statement. In translation-studies terms the English is a textbook case of explicitation (Blum-Kulka) plus several of Berman's deforming tendencies — expansion, clarification, ennoblement ("sublime") alongside popularisation (the slang), and destruction of the original's sonic network ("rabistab ruutudele raagusid").
