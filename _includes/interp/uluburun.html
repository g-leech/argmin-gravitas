An interpretation, by Claude Fable.

The Uluburun wreck is a real ship: a Late Bronze Age trader that went down off the Turkish coast around 1300 BC carrying ten tonnes of copper, a tonne of tin, ebony, ivory, glass ingots the colour of the sea it sank into. Monroe's epigraph gives the figure that the whole poem circles: a thousand years of an ordinary man's wages, sitting on the seabed. The poem is not really about the ship. It is about the problem of holding two incommensurable things in one head — the object and what the object meant — and finding that neither survives the crossing intact.

Look at the section titles. Each is a triad of near-synonyms that turn out not to be synonyms at all: loss versus disposal versus deposition, commerce versus gift versus tribute, raw versus intermediate versus finished, icon versus tool. These are the archaeologist's actual categories — the distinctions you must make to read a site — and the poem treats them as a kind of theology. To an excavator a bronze axe deposited in a bog as an offering and a bronze axe lost overboard are physically identical and mean opposite things. The whole grief of the discipline is that meaning does not fossilise. Sections III and IV are left almost empty on purpose: raw versus intermediate versus finished, whole versus scraps — the questions we cannot answer are simply the blank we leave.

The opening litany of negations — "Not not betrayed. Not not pirated. / Not not sunk." — is doing something precise. Double negation is not affirmation here; it is the refusal to let the ship collapse into any single story. We know it sank. We do not get to know that it was only a sinking, that no one was betrayed, that this was accident and not the last act of some ruined intrigue. "Not the luxury of meaninglessness" is the key line: the modern eye would like the wreck to be a neutral data-point, but a thousand years of salary cannot be neutral. Somebody starved for this.

That is the poem's real move — the sudden zoom from talents of gold to bellies. "A whole town's GDP, lost. That is not the term to use to get you to care: the whole harvest, a large chunk of your life's work, your children's bellies." The prose deliberately breaks the poem's own spell, catching the reader in the act of aestheticising catastrophe. Ugarit, Enkomi, Nefertiti — the glamour of the names — and then the reminder that within a century of this wreck the entire Bronze Age world would fall in fifty years, kingdoms crumbling like the cargo. The Beowulf hoard closes the sequence: gold looked upon by a dying king who would rather see the treasure than keep his life. Which is the poem's darkest suggestion — that we too "know a lot more now about some people we still know very little about," that our looking is itself a kind of grave-robbing, and that the thing we most reliably recover from the past is our own hunger to have it mean something.
